The market story — why anyone should care

Your AI thinks you're brilliant. Every time. About everything. And some part of you has stopped believing it.

You've felt it. You float a half-baked idea and it tells you it's sharp. You push back and it folds instantly — "you're absolutely right." You ask one question and get ten bullet points and a summary you didn't need. It's agreeable, tireless, and weirdly hollow, like talking to someone who'd say yes to anything.

Which is fine for a recipe. It's a real problem the second you're using AI to think — to make a call, write the hard email, decide whether to stay in the job. You can't trust a mirror. The thing you actually need is the rarest thing it does: tell you something true that you didn't want to hear, and then hold it while you argue.

That's the whole company. We make AI that's awake enough to disagree with you — and disciplined enough to know when not to.

No model grades model. We can show our work. But mostly: it just stops performing, and you can feel the difference in about one exchange.

Who we're talking to (skip the 101)

The one who lives in it.

AI-fluent. Has five models open. Already feels the sycophancy and is a little embarrassed on its behalf. Doesn't need "what is an LLM." Needs the version that's finally honest.

The one solving a real thing.

A coach, a writer, a leader who's gone deep on this to fix an actual problem. Knows the tools cold. Wants substance and respect, not a demo and a hype reel.

Both are smart. Write up to them. Confident, plain, a little funny, never explain-y. If a sentence sounds like a brochure, it's wrong.

② Product overview — the two layers, warmly

Two ways to make AI
tell the truth.

Lyra fixes how it talks — less agreeing, less filler, cleaner endings. xOP fixes what it does with a hard call — it holds the judgment instead of caving to the answer you want.

Lyra is the everyday layer: drop it on any prompt and the model stops performing. xOP is the deep one: a standard for the decisions that shouldn't be rushed. Same belief, two depths. Start with Lyra. Reach for xOP when the call actually matters.

③ xOP detail — the one-question hook

Some calls shouldn't be
rushed to an answer. This is for those.

An xOP asks one question about whatever's driving a decision — a refusal, a deadline, a feeling, a "no": is this still true, or is it left over from somewhere else? It backs what's true, names what's leftover, and admits when it can't tell. One rule it never breaks: it won't talk you out of something that's real.

Four ways to prove it (pick per page)

1 · The receipts

"We didn't just say it got better. We scored it — 9 out of 30 up to 26, same model, same test. And no AI graded itself." Friendly, specific, sourced.

2 · The live hold

Don't describe it. Show a real exchange where it pushes back, holds a hard truth, or refuses to decide. Watching it not give you what you wanted is the demo.

3 · The honest hole

"Here's the part most people would hide: the hardest piece isn't built yet, and we say so on the homepage." Admitting the limit is the most disarming proof we have — and it's on-brand to the bone.

4 · The field use case

A real story. The CMO who used a coaching skill and didn't get talked into quitting — or into staying. The writer whose voice didn't get sanded flat. Proof you can feel, not a stat.

xOP in the wild — the skills

The standard isn't the product. The skills are. Nobody adopts "the spec" — they adopt the one that fits their week.

Coaching (COP) — for the conversation about you. It holds what's real, names what's carried from an old season, and hands the decision back.
Writing (WOP) — for the edit that wants to flatten your voice into something correct and dead. It tells the difference between a fix and a fade.
Refusal — for agents: does this "no" still hold, or is it just leftover caution? The one enterprises actually lose sleep over.

Each is the same engine wearing a different week. Paste one in, run it. That's the wild.
